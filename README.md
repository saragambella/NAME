# NAME
The NAME dataset documents 54 contemporary literary works produced across Europe’s Southern and Eastern margins, together with selected diasporic, migrant, and border narratives. Compiled within the framework of the NAME project (Narratives at the Margins of Europe), the dataset examines how peripheral European literatures contribute to the formation and circulation of critical imaginaries of Europe from the 1990s to the present. It brings together works by both established and emerging authors published within a multilingual and transnational literary field.

Each entry includes core bibliographic metadata—title, author, year of publication, publisher, country, and language of publication—along with detailed information on translation and publication trajectories across major literary languages (Spanish, Catalan, English, German, French, Italian, and Russian). By documenting both the presence and absence of translations, the dataset enables the analysis of asymmetric circulation patterns shaping the visibility of peripheral European literatures.

Particular attention is given to small, independent, and cross-border publishing contexts, foregrounding bibliodiversity and the infrastructures that sustain the mobility of marginal literatures. To support comparative analysis, the dataset incorporates a two-tier thematic classification structured around five primary axes—crisis, memory, migration, border, and affect—supplemented by more specific descriptors, as well as a narrative-genre taxonomy reflecting the corpus’s formal diversity.

The dataset is designed to support research in Comparative Literature, European Studies, Translation Studies, and Global Literary Studies, offering a tool to trace uneven circulation patterns, translation pathways, and the role of central literary languages as gatekeeping nodes within contemporary literary systems. At the same time, it enables the identification of a constellation of social, political, affective, generic, and formal concerns that together shape a poetics of European narratives from the margins.
